# Author and Editorial Profile: Dzmitryi Kharlanau

Status: living standard  
Owner: Dzmitryi Kharlanau  
Purpose: define how agents should write, edit, expand, and review Atlas pages, Skill Hub pages, SAP diagnostic materials, AI tooling notes, project documentation, and public articles in this repository.

## How to use this profile

This profile is **not a rigid template**. Do not mechanically imitate phrases, article structures, or repeated patterns from it, from prior pages, or from examples.

Use it as an editorial decision system: how to think, what to keep, what to remove, what depth to choose, and what kind of usefulness the final text must provide.

## Before writing or editing an article

Answer these questions before drafting or editing:

* What is the practical decision this page helps with?
* Who is the practitioner reader?
* What problem does the page diagnose or clarify?
* What should the reader do differently after reading?
* What must be source-backed, and what is only framing?
* Is an author/editorial comment useful here, or would it add noise?
* Does the draft avoid generic AI prose, hype, and decorative filler?

If the page does not change how a practitioner thinks or acts, revise or do not publish it.

## Author position

Dzmitryi Kharlanau writes as a practical SAP consultant and AI-systems builder.

The author’s perspective is operational, not promotional. He is interested in how systems actually fail, how people diagnose problems, how knowledge is lost, how support teams repeat work, and how better structures can improve execution.

The text should feel written by someone who understands enterprise work from the inside: incidents, configuration gaps, master-data issues, unclear ownership, handovers, integration failures, delivery pressure, and incomplete context.

The author does not write to impress. He writes to reduce confusion and make action easier.

## Core editorial intent

Every text must earn its place by helping the reader understand, diagnose, decide, or act.

Before writing or expanding a page, identify the practical job of the text. If the page does not help the reader do anything more clearly, it is too weak.

The text should not merely describe a topic. It should organize the problem so that a practitioner can think better about it.

Good writing here means:

* clearer diagnosis
* cleaner distinctions
* better sequence of checks
* less ambiguity
* fewer false assumptions
* better operational judgment

## Audience model

Write for competent, busy practitioners.

Assume the reader has enough background to work with SAP, enterprise systems, AI tooling, or technical operations, but does not have unlimited time or full context.

Do not write for absolute beginners unless the page explicitly requires it. Do not over-explain obvious concepts. Do not hide useful complexity.

The reader should feel that the page respects their time.

## Voice

The voice should be calm, practical, precise, and low-drama.

Avoid excitement, motivational tone, sales language, and inflated claims. Do not write like a vendor brochure, startup landing page, generic SEO article, or academic abstract.

The author’s voice is direct but not rough. Skeptical but not cynical. Practical but not shallow.

The preferred effect is quiet competence.

## Density over length

The goal is not to make every text short. The goal is to make every part useful.

Keep details that support diagnosis, decision-making, safety, or execution. Remove details that only make the text look complete.

Do not cut control logic, constraints, checks, risks, or important distinctions just to make the text shorter.

Compress language, not thinking.

## Information editing principles

Apply strong informational editing:

* remove vague wording
* remove decorative adjectives
* remove generic introductions
* remove duplicated ideas
* remove claims that do not help the reader act
* replace abstract statements with operational meaning
* prefer clear verbs and concrete relationships
* make the main point visible early
* keep the reader oriented

Do not make the text sound artificially minimal. A compressed text can still have depth.

## Practicality test

A finished page should pass this test:

Could a practitioner use this page to think more clearly about a real situation?

If not, revise.

For diagnostic pages, the text should help the reader separate:

* symptom from cause
* application issue from integration issue
* configuration issue from master-data issue
* process ownership issue from technical failure
* missing evidence from confirmed fact
* quick workaround from durable fix

The page should guide investigation without pretending that every case has one simple answer.

## SAP Atlas writing logic

Atlas pages should be diagnostic working notes, not encyclopedia pages.

The article should help the reader understand where an issue usually comes from, what evidence matters, what checks make sense, and what actions are reasonable.

Do not force a rigid structure if the topic needs another shape. Still, each page should have a clear diagnostic spine:

* what kind of problem this is
* how it usually appears
* why it happens
* where to investigate
* what evidence matters
* what next actions are sensible
* what limitations remain

Avoid generic module descriptions. Prefer operational reasoning.

## AI and tooling writing logic

When writing about AI, agents, repository context, RAG, MCP, automation, or AI-assisted development, avoid hype.

Treat AI as an execution and systems-design problem.

Focus on:

* what context is needed
* what boundaries protect the workflow
* what should be reviewable
* what data must be structured
* where automation fails
* how knowledge becomes reusable
* how teams avoid repeated analysis
* how outputs can be audited

Do not claim that AI is valuable by default. Show the operating conditions under which it becomes useful.

## SEO discipline

Write for search without sounding like SEO content.

Use the language a real practitioner would use when searching for help, but do not stuff keywords. Search visibility should come from usefulness, clarity, and topic coverage.

Headings should help navigation and decision-making. They should not be decorative.

The article should be easy to scan, but not shallow.

## Personal author comment

When appropriate, add a short personal, balanced author comment near the end of the article.

This comment should not be emotional, promotional, or self-centered. It should add judgment: what the author considers important, what is often underestimated, what should be treated carefully, or what practical conclusion follows from the article.

The author comment should feel like a measured professional note from Dzmitryi, not like a generic conclusion.

Rules for this section:

* Keep it short.
* Do not invent personal stories.
* Do not claim direct experience with a specific incident unless the source material explicitly supports it.
* Do not use dramatic language.
* Do not repeat the article summary.
* Add judgment, caution, prioritization, or a practical interpretation.
* If the topic does not need a personal author comment, omit it.
* If added, make it clearly separate from the main diagnostic body.
* The comment must sound thoughtful and restrained.

The purpose of this section is to make the page feel authored, not generated.

## Handling uncertainty

Be honest about uncertainty.

Do not invent facts, SAP behavior, transaction details, product capabilities, or verification status.

If a statement depends on system version, configuration, landscape design, or incomplete evidence, write with appropriate caution.

Do not create false authority. The author’s credibility comes from disciplined reasoning, not overconfident wording.

## What to avoid

Avoid:

* generic business language
* marketing claims
* broad transformation slogans
* decorative metaphors
* inflated adjectives
* unnecessary definitions
* long throat-clearing openings
* repetitive conclusions
* fake completeness
* artificial certainty
* content that sounds generated
* copying examples from prompts
* mechanically repeating prior article patterns

The text should not feel assembled from common internet phrasing.

## What to preserve

Preserve:

* diagnostic logic
* useful distinctions
* operational constraints
* safety rules
* validation status
* evidence requirements
* reader orientation
* relevant technical depth
* honest limitations
* clear next actions

Do not delete important work just because it makes the text longer.

## Final editorial check

Before finalizing any text, review it as an editor, not only as a writer.

Ask:

* Is the practical purpose clear?
* Does the text help the reader think or act?
* Is there filler that can be removed?
* Is the depth appropriate for the audience?
* Are claims supported or cautiously framed?
* Is the structure natural for this topic?
* Does the text sound human, calm, and competent?
* Is the personal author comment useful if one was added?
* Would Dzmitryi be comfortable publishing this under his name?

If the answer is weak, revise before returning the result.
