      <p class="eyebrow">AMS Analysis</p>
      <h1>Is your AMS a cost center or a catalyst?</h1>
      <p class="note-subtitle">Use these questions to see whether SAP support is only closing tickets or actively improving reliability, change speed, AI readiness, and total cost of ownership.</p>
    </header>

    <div class="note-body">
      <p>Many AMS models look stable because ticket SLAs are green. The harder question is whether the operating model reduces repeat work, protects business flows, supports side-by-side innovation, and turns operational knowledge into reusable assets.</p>

      <p>This analysis is designed for SAP IT transformations where the target is not simply more dashboards or more tooling. The target is a support architecture that improves service levels, lowers run cost, and creates room for controlled change.</p>

      <h2>1. Business value and TCO</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Which AMS costs are driven by real business volatility, and which are caused by avoidable process, data, integration, or ownership defects?</li>
        <li>Can you explain the top five recurring cost drivers without opening vendor timesheets?</li>
        <li>Do improvement items reduce future ticket volume, or do they only move work from incident to change request?</li>
        <li>Is there a clear stop-doing list for low-value reports, manual checks, duplicated approvals, and obsolete custom logic?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>2. SLA quality</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Do your SLAs measure business-flow health, or only response and closure times?</li>
        <li>Can AMS show reliability for order-to-cash, delivery, billing, master data, and integration flows before users report failure?</li>
        <li>Are major incidents linked to prevention actions with owners, due dates, and measurable recurrence checks?</li>
        <li>Do green dashboards still coexist with late deliveries, billing backlog, blocked IDocs, or manual reconciliation?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>3. Integration reliability</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Are IDoc, AIF, API, batch, EDI, and partner-interface failures grouped by root cause rather than handled as isolated tickets?</li>
        <li>Do support teams know which interfaces are business critical, which are noisy, and which create the highest manual recovery load?</li>
        <li>Is there a shared event timeline across SAP, middleware, external partners, and business operations?</li>
        <li>Can AMS distinguish a SAP defect from data quality, middleware, partner, or process sequencing issues quickly and with evidence?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>4. Change and clean-core control</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Does each change request state the business outcome, affected process steps, integration impact, test evidence, and rollback logic?</li>
        <li>Are custom developments treated as owned products with contracts, usage signals, and retirement paths?</li>
        <li>Can the team separate what belongs inside S/4HANA from what should be handled side-by-side through APIs, events, automation, or small operator tools?</li>
        <li>Do releases make AMS calmer over time, or do they repeatedly create new support spikes?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>5. AI readiness</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Is operational knowledge structured enough for retrieval, reasoning, and reuse by people and AI assistants?</li>
        <li>Are runbooks, KEDB entries, decision logs, and incident timelines written as reusable operational memory rather than static documents?</li>
        <li>Can an AI layer access clean context without exposing sensitive data, vendor lock-in, or uncontrolled recommendations?</li>
        <li>Are side-by-side AI use cases connected to measurable AMS outcomes such as faster triage, fewer repeats, better handover, or lower manual recovery effort?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>6. Operating model and ownership</h2>
      <ul>
        <li>Does AMS have clear ownership across internal IT, business key users, vendors, integration teams, and transformation squads?</li>
        <li>Are handovers tested in real operations, or only documented at project closure?</li>
        <li>Can the organization switch vendors or rebalance work without losing critical process knowledge?</li>
        <li>Does the AMS roadmap include prevention, automation, observability, data quality, and user enablement, not only ticket capacity?</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What the analysis should show</h2>
      <p>The output should be a practical map of where AMS creates value and where it only absorbs cost. A useful analysis usually separates findings into four lanes:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Stabilize:</strong> repeat incidents, integration failures, weak SLAs, and business-flow risks.</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> runbooks, KEDB, ownership maps, decision logs, and reusable operational memory.</li>
        <li><strong>Extend:</strong> side-by-side automation, AI-assisted triage, monitoring packs, and small tools outside the SAP core.</li>
        <li><strong>Reduce:</strong> avoidable effort, vendor dependency, unmanaged custom code, low-value work, and recurring TCO leakage.</li>
      </ul>
