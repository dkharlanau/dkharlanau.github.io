# How to Reduce Warehouse Costs with SAP EWM Without Slowing Order Fulfilment

A warehouse reports a productivity improvement.

Lines picked per hour increased by 12%.

Labour cost per order decreased.

Management celebrates.

At the same time:

- more orders miss carrier cut-off;
- packing queues become longer;
- urgent orders wait behind large waves;
- replenishment shortages stop picking;
- customer deliveries are split;
- overtime moves from picking to packing and staging.

The warehouse has improved one productivity measure.

The fulfilment process has become more expensive.

This is the trap in warehouse optimization.

Managers frequently ask:

> How can we make warehouse workers faster?

The better question is:

> Why does the warehouse require so much movement, waiting, handling and correction to fulfil one customer order?

Warehouse cost is rarely caused only by individual worker productivity.

It is generated by the complete operating design:

- order profile;
- release timing;
- storage layout;
- product placement;
- replenishment;
- packaging;
- inventory accuracy;
- transport schedules;
- master data;
- exception policy.

A warehouse can have skilled employees, modern automation and SAP EWM and still operate inefficiently because the work itself has been designed badly.

My position is direct:

> Do not begin warehouse optimization by measuring how fast people work. Begin by measuring how much unnecessary work the system creates for them.

SAP EWM currently supports high-volume warehouse operations, stock and process transparency, intelligent slotting, wave-based outbound processing, labour structures, yard management, cross-docking and direct integration with warehouse automation and robotics.

These capabilities are valuable.

They do not determine which orders should be grouped, where products should be stored or which exceptions deserve manual effort.

That remains a management and process-design responsibility.

## Warehouse cost is produced by physical behaviour

SAP documents and warehouse tasks are digital representations.

The cost comes from physical activity.

Typical cost drivers include:

- walking or driving;
- searching;
- lifting;
- opening and closing handling units;
- replenishing;
- counting;
- waiting;
- sorting;
- consolidating;
- correcting;
- repacking;
- moving goods twice.

A useful warehouse diagnostic should therefore begin with physical behaviour.

For one typical order, ask:

1. How far does the picker travel?
2. How many storage locations are visited?
3. How many times is each unit touched?
4. How long does the order wait between stages?
5. How many replenishments are triggered?
6. How often does the planned process fail?
7. How much work exists only because of an exception?

This is more informative than beginning with a list of SAP transactions.

## The warehouse should not compensate for commercial disorder

Warehouse operations are affected by decisions made before an order reaches the warehouse.

Examples include:

- late sales-order changes;
- unrealistic delivery dates;
- unnecessary partial-delivery permissions;
- inconsistent order priorities;
- incorrect packaging requirements;
- low minimum-order values;
- missing customer instructions;
- customer-specific labelling added at the last moment.

The warehouse then pays through:

- priority changes;
- additional waves;
- order splitting;
- manual packing;
- staging changes;
- premium labour;
- missed transport cut-offs.

A warehouse optimization programme should therefore measure the quality of the demand entering the warehouse.

### Important upstream measures

- percentage of orders changed after release;
- percentage of orders requiring manual priority;
- percentage of orders with missing warehouse-relevant data;
- percentage of orders released outside the planned schedule;
- percentage of customer-specific packaging instructions added late;
- number of delivery splits caused by commercial rules.

A warehouse cannot become stable if the demand entering it remains unstable.

## Start with order-profile economics

Not all warehouse orders create the same work.

Consider four orders.

### Order A

- one full pallet;
- one product;
- standard label;
- direct loading.

### Order B

- 80 pieces;
- 20 products;
- several storage zones;
- individual picking;
- special packaging.

### Order C

- three products;
- dangerous-goods documentation;
- export labelling;
- quality release.

### Order D

- one urgent spare part;
- immediate picking;
- premium transport.

Counting all four as one order hides the real workload.

### Segment warehouse demand

Useful dimensions include:

- full pallet;
- case;
- piece;
- single-line;
- multi-line;
- small order;
- urgent order;
- export;
- customer-specific value-added service;
- temperature controlled;
- dangerous goods;
- returns.

For each profile, calculate:

- average touches;
- travel;
- processing time;
- packaging;
- labour;
- error rate;
- waiting;
- exception rate.

This creates a warehouse cost-to-serve model.

It also shows which order patterns should be:

- priced differently;
- consolidated;
- automated;
- redesigned;
- commercially restricted.

## Stop using one average productivity number

“Lines per hour” is useful only when the work is comparable.

A picker processing full cases in one zone cannot be compared directly with a picker handling:

- small pieces;
- serial numbers;
- batch restrictions;
- heavy goods;
- customer-specific labelling.

If management pushes one productivity target across all work, employees and supervisors will optimize the metric.

Possible behaviour includes:

- selecting easier work first;
- delaying complex orders;
- creating more partial tasks;
- sacrificing accuracy;
- moving effort to another department.

Use productivity measures by process archetype.

For example:

- pallet picks per hour;
- case lines per hour;
- piece lines per hour;
- replenishment tasks per hour;
- packing minutes by order profile;
- exception minutes per case.

The objective is not to create a complex measurement bureaucracy.

It is to avoid managing unlike work as if it were identical.

## Measure flow time, not only working time

An order may require 20 minutes of active warehouse work but remain in the warehouse for 12 hours.

The remaining time may consist of:

- waiting for release;
- waiting for picking;
- waiting for replenishment;
- waiting for packing;
- waiting for staging;
- waiting for transport.

The customer experiences the full lead time.

### Separate active and waiting time

For each order profile, measure:

```text
Order available
→ delivery released
→ picking started
→ picking completed
→ packing completed
→ staging completed
→ loaded
```

This reveals queues.

A faster picking operation may have limited value when orders wait six hours before picking begins.

## Warehouse optimization lever 1: control order release

Releasing more work does not automatically increase throughput.

It may create:

- too many open tasks;
- congestion;
- competition for stock;
- replenishment pressure;
- staging overload;
- loss of priority visibility.

A warehouse should release work according to:

- required departure;
- service priority;
- resource availability;
- stock readiness;
- picking zone capacity;
- packing capacity;
- staging capacity;
- carrier cut-off.

SAP EWM supports outbound scheduling through waves and optimized work packages. It also supports connections between warehouse execution, transportation, production and yard operations.

The system can group and release work.

Management must decide how the warehouse should balance efficiency and urgency.

### A wave should have an economic purpose

A wave may be designed around:

- carrier departure;
- route;
- customer;
- warehouse zone;
- product profile;
- priority;
- labour shift.

Do not create waves simply because the functionality exists.

Every wave should answer:

> Which operational advantage are we creating by grouping these orders?

Possible advantages include:

- reduced travel;
- consolidated packing;
- synchronized loading;
- balanced labour;
- protected cut-off.

### Large waves are not always better

Large waves can improve picking density.

They can also cause:

- a large replenishment peak;
- packing congestion;
- blocked staging;
- delayed urgent orders;
- difficult recovery after interruption.

Measure the full downstream effect.

## Warehouse optimization lever 2: improve slotting

Slotting determines where products are stored.

Poor slotting creates permanent travel cost.

A fast-moving product placed far from packing generates repeated waste every day.

### Slotting should consider more than movement frequency

Relevant factors may include:

- demand frequency;
- quantity per pick;
- product dimensions;
- weight;
- order affinity;
- storage requirements;
- replenishment frequency;
- product seasonality;
- handling restrictions;
- packaging unit;
- ergonomic constraints.

SAP EWM currently includes intelligent slotting rules intended to improve space utilization, along with stock, resource and process transparency.

### Do not use a static ABC classification forever

Demand changes.

A product may move from slow to fast because of:

- season;
- customer launch;
- promotion;
- product phase-out;
- changed distribution strategy.

Slotting should be reviewed periodically.

But relocation also costs money.

The decision should compare:

```text
Expected future travel saving
–
Relocation and disruption cost
```

Do not move products because the theoretical model changed slightly.

Move them when the expected saving justifies the action.

### Use order affinity

Products frequently ordered together may benefit from being stored near one another or inside a compatible picking flow.

This can reduce:

- travel;
- consolidation;
- sorting.

But order affinity should not override:

- safety;
- weight;
- storage conditions;
- replenishment constraints.

## Warehouse optimization lever 3: fix replenishment

Picking often stops because the reserve stock exists but the pick face is empty.

This creates:

- picker waiting;
- emergency replenishment;
- task interruption;
- supervisor intervention;
- missed wave completion.

Replenishment is not a secondary warehouse process.

It is the supply process for picking.

### Measure replenishment reliability

Useful metrics include:

- percentage of picks stopped by empty locations;
- emergency replenishments;
- replenishment tasks created too late;
- pick faces overfilled;
- replenishments later reversed or moved;
- travel per replenishment.

### Separate replenishment strategies

Possible approaches include:

#### Demand-based replenishment

Triggered by released or expected warehouse work.

#### Minimum–maximum replenishment

Triggered by stock thresholds.

#### Scheduled replenishment

Performed before a shift or wave.

#### Predictive replenishment

Uses expected workload and consumption.

The correct approach may differ by product class.

### Avoid excessive pick-face inventory

Large pick faces reduce replenishment frequency.

They may increase:

- space consumption;
- travel distance;
- slow-moving stock in prime locations;
- inventory counting effort.

The correct pick-face size balances:

- picking demand;
- replenishment cost;
- available space;
- service risk.

## Warehouse optimization lever 4: reduce travel

Travel is often one of the largest components of picking effort.

Possible reduction methods include:

- better slotting;
- zone picking;
- batch picking;
- multi-order picking;
- route sequencing;
- pick-and-pass;
- goods-to-person automation.

SAP EWM supports flexible picking across multiple customer orders in a single run, wave-based outbound activities and integration with automation.

### Batch picking can move cost rather than remove it

Picking several orders together reduces travel.

It may require later sorting.

The total saving is:

```text
Reduced picking travel
–
Additional sorting and consolidation
–
Additional error risk
```

For small e-commerce orders, batch picking may work well.

For complex or high-value orders, the later separation may cost more than the saved travel.

### Zone picking needs balanced workloads

If one order requires products from several zones, the slowest zone may determine completion.

The warehouse should monitor:

- workload by zone;
- completion synchronization;
- waiting at consolidation;
- product placement causing imbalance.

## Warehouse optimization lever 5: reduce touches

Every touch creates cost and risk.

Consider an inbound pallet that is:

1. unloaded;
2. moved to receiving;
3. moved to quality;
4. moved to temporary storage;
5. put away;
6. replenished;
7. picked;
8. moved to consolidation;
9. moved to packing;
10. moved to staging;
11. loaded.

Some touches are required.

Some exist because the flow is fragmented.

### Look for flow-through opportunities

Possible patterns include:

- cross-docking;
- direct putaway;
- direct production supply;
- direct receipt to consumption;
- pre-packed supplier units;
- ship-ready handling units.

SAP EWM supports cross-docking, direct receipt scenarios, production integration and detailed handling of stock and warehouse processes.

### Cross-docking is not a universal solution

It works when:

- inbound supply and outbound demand are aligned;
- product identity is reliable;
- timing is predictable;
- quality and regulatory controls permit it.

If timing is unstable, cross-docking may create:

- staging congestion;
- missing outbound quantities;
- manual matching.

## Warehouse optimization lever 6: manage congestion

A warehouse has physical bottlenecks.

Examples include:

- receiving docks;
- narrow aisles;
- lifts;
- packing stations;
- sorters;
- staging lanes;
- loading docks.

Increasing labour before one bottleneck may only increase the queue.

### Find the actual constraint

Observe throughput and waiting at each stage.

For example:

- picking is fast;
- packing cannot process the volume;
- completed picks occupy carts;
- aisles become blocked;
- picking productivity later falls.

Hiring more pickers would make the problem worse.

### Capacity should be measured by time window

A warehouse may have sufficient daily capacity but insufficient capacity at 15:00 before carrier cut-off.

Measure capacity by:

- hour;
- shift;
- zone;
- resource;
- process stage.

Daily averages hide operational peaks.

## Warehouse optimization lever 7: balance labour by workload

Labour planning should reflect the expected work mix.

The warehouse may need different skills for:

- pallet handling;
- piece picking;
- dangerous goods;
- packing;
- quality;
- equipment operation.

SAP EWM’s advanced capabilities include labour structures and standards for tracking, as well as resource and process transparency.

### Labour standards should support diagnosis, not punishment

A standard time can help:

- estimate workload;
- plan shifts;
- compare process variants;
- find abnormal delays.

It becomes destructive when used without context.

Differences may be caused by:

- poor slotting;
- equipment delay;
- system response;
- replenishment failure;
- difficult order profile;
- inaccurate master data.

Do not turn every variance into an employee-performance problem.

### Measure productive availability

Scheduled labour is not the same as productive capacity.

Time may be lost to:

- shift start;
- equipment collection;
- battery change;
- meetings;
- system outage;
- waiting;
- search;
- reassignment.

The objective is not to eliminate every non-picking minute.

It is to understand where capacity is consumed.

## Warehouse optimization lever 8: improve inventory accuracy

A warehouse task based on incorrect stock creates:

- search;
- short pick;
- task cancellation;
- alternative location;
- customer shortage;
- recounting.

Inventory accuracy is therefore a productivity measure, not only an accounting control.

### Measure accuracy by operational importance

A total accuracy of 99.8% may hide serious errors in:

- fast-moving products;
- expensive products;
- critical spare parts;
- batches;
- serial numbers.

Use risk-based cycle counting.

SAP EWM supports physical inventory, cycle counting and visibility across stock ownership and warehouse locations.

### Fix the transaction causing the difference

Counting corrects the record.

It does not correct the process.

Investigate whether differences originate in:

- receiving;
- unit conversion;
- picking confirmation;
- damaged goods;
- unrecorded movement;
- interface delay;
- handling-unit process.

If the same difference returns, counting is only maintenance of a defect.

## Warehouse optimization lever 9: improve packaging

Packing is often treated as the final simple step.

It can become a major bottleneck.

Cost drivers include:

- excessive packaging choice;
- customer-specific labels;
- repacking;
- missing dimensions;
- incorrect packaging instructions;
- manual document preparation.

### Standardize packaging where commercially possible

Review:

- number of packaging variants;
- customer-specific requirements;
- product protection;
- transport utilization;
- sustainability;
- packing time.

Customer-specific packaging should have visible cost.

Otherwise, sales can promise it without understanding the warehouse impact.

### Packaging data affects transportation

Incorrect dimensions and weight can lead to:

- poor load planning;
- incorrect freight rates;
- capacity problems;
- repacking.

Warehouse and transportation optimization should share one packaging model.

## Warehouse optimization lever 10: control returns

Returns can disrupt the normal flow.

Returned goods require:

- identification;
- inspection;
- disposition;
- repacking;
- storage;
- credit or replacement.

SAP EWM currently includes capabilities aimed at streamlining e-commerce returns and managing customer-initiated changes and cancellations.

### Segment return reasons

Examples include:

- customer changed mind;
- wrong item;
- damage;
- delivery failure;
- product defect;
- incorrect quantity;
- commercial return.

The warehouse should not carry the cost of defects caused by:

- sales;
- product quality;
- packing;
- transport;
- customer policy.

Classify the initiating cause.

### Accelerate disposition

Returned inventory has uncertain value until a decision is made.

Possible states include:

- return to available stock;
- repair;
- rework;
- secondary sale;
- supplier return;
- scrap.

Slow disposition consumes space and hides usable inventory.

## Warehouse optimization lever 11: design exceptions separately

A warehouse should have a stable standard flow.

Exceptions should not reshape the standard process every day.

Common exceptions include:

- urgent order;
- stock mismatch;
- damaged item;
- missing label;
- unavailable packaging;
- blocked batch;
- equipment failure;
- carrier delay.

### Create an exception-control model

Every exception should include:

- business reference;
- cause;
- priority;
- impact;
- owner;
- permitted actions;
- deadline.

### Do not use supervisors as a permanent integration layer

In many warehouses, supervisors solve problems through:

- phone calls;
- emails;
- spreadsheets;
- manual priority changes.

This gives the operation flexibility.

It also hides structural defects.

Record the reason for manual intervention.

Repeated interventions should produce changes in:

- master data;
- process;
- interface;
- commercial policy;
- layout.

## Automation should follow process stability

Warehouse automation can include:

- conveyors;
- sorters;
- automated storage;
- autonomous mobile robots;
- robotic picking;
- voice;
- augmented-reality picking.

SAP EWM can directly control warehouse automation equipment, while SAP Warehouse Robotics supports onboarding and coordinating robotics vendors.

Automation can provide value.

It can also automate a poor process and make changes more expensive.

### Ask five questions before automation

1. Is the volume sufficiently stable?
2. Is the process standardized?
3. Is product and packaging data reliable?
4. What percentage of work follows the standard path?
5. What happens when automation stops?

### Automate repeatable flow

Good candidates often have:

- stable product profile;
- predictable volume;
- high repetition;
- clear handling rules;
- measurable manual cost.

### Do not automate around bad master data

Robots and conveyors depend on:

- dimensions;
- weight;
- handling-unit structure;
- location;
- routing;
- status.

A person may improvise when data is wrong.

Automation normally cannot.

### Include exception labour in the business case

Automation may reduce standard labour.

It may require:

- maintenance;
- control-room support;
- exception handling;
- manual fallback;
- specialist skills.

The real benefit is:

```text
Removed manual work
–
Automation operating cost
–
Maintenance
–
Exception and fallback cost
```

## Calculate ROI from removed physical work

A credible warehouse business case should identify which activity disappears.

### Travel reduction

```text
Reduced travel minutes per line
× annual lines
× loaded labour rate
```

### Touch reduction

```text
Removed touches per unit
× annual units
× average handling time
× loaded labour rate
```

### Error reduction

```text
Reduced picking errors
× average correction, return and redelivery cost
```

### Space improvement

```text
Avoided or released storage area
× annual space cost
```

### Throughput benefit

May include:

- avoided overtime;
- avoided temporary labour;
- delayed warehouse expansion;
- increased volume capacity.

### Automation value

Subtract:

- capital cost;
- software;
- maintenance;
- energy;
- support;
- exception labour;
- fallback capability.

Do not assume that every saved minute becomes a real cash saving.

Labour benefit becomes cash only when the company can:

- reduce overtime;
- avoid hiring;
- reassign capacity;
- process more volume.

## A strong first pilot

A good pilot could be:

> Reduce picking travel and late completion for standard multi-line orders in one warehouse zone.

### Scope

- one product family;
- one picking zone;
- standard orders;
- one shift;
- no major automation investment.

### Baseline

Measure:

- order lines;
- route distance;
- time per line;
- replenishment interruptions;
- short picks;
- completion before cut-off;
- packing waiting;
- labour.

### Diagnose

Identify:

- fast movers in poor locations;
- products frequently ordered together;
- oversized pick faces;
- products creating emergency replenishment;
- unbalanced work release.

### Interventions

1. update slotting for selected products;
2. resize pick faces;
3. introduce planned replenishment before the main wave;
4. adjust release timing;
5. test multi-order picking where suitable;
6. track downstream packing congestion.

### Guardrails

Do not accept the pilot if:

- pick accuracy declines;
- packing queues rise materially;
- urgent orders miss cut-off;
- replenishment labour cancels the travel saving.

### Prove the economics

Show:

- travel minutes removed;
- labour capacity released;
- error change;
- service change;
- relocation cost;
- sustainability of the result.

## A practical programme sequence

### Phase 1: Segment warehouse demand

Separate order and product archetypes.

### Phase 2: Establish physical-flow baseline

Measure movement, touches, waiting and exceptions.

### Phase 3: Find the real bottleneck

Do not assume picking is the constraint.

### Phase 4: Stabilize order release

Control workload entering the warehouse.

### Phase 5: Correct master data

Fix dimensions, UoM, packaging and storage data.

### Phase 6: Improve slotting and replenishment

Reduce repeated travel and pick interruption.

### Phase 7: Balance labour and capacity

Plan by process, zone and time window.

### Phase 8: Reduce touches and queues

Simplify the physical flow.

### Phase 9: Control exceptions

Record and remove recurring causes.

### Phase 10: Introduce targeted automation

Automate stable, high-volume work.

### Phase 11: Connect warehouse and transport

Align completion with carrier departures.

### Phase 12: Scale by warehouse archetype

Do not copy one design into warehouses with different demand profiles.

## Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Managing only lines per hour

Waiting, quality and service deteriorate.

### Mistake 2: Blaming employees for process waste

Poor slotting and replenishment remain unchanged.

### Mistake 3: Releasing all work immediately

The warehouse fills with open tasks and congestion.

### Mistake 4: Creating very large waves

Picking improves while packing and staging collapse.

### Mistake 5: Using static slotting

Demand changes but prime locations remain occupied by slow movers.

### Mistake 6: Reducing replenishment frequency by enlarging every pick face

Prime space is consumed by excess stock.

### Mistake 7: Automating before standardizing

Robotics inherit unstable rules and poor data.

### Mistake 8: Measuring daily capacity only

Hourly cut-off bottlenecks remain hidden.

### Mistake 9: Treating inventory counting as the solution

The transaction creating the difference is not corrected.

### Mistake 10: Hiding customer-specific work

Packaging and labelling cost remains commercially invisible.

### Mistake 11: Optimizing picking without packing

Cost moves to the next process.

### Mistake 12: Treating every return as a warehouse failure

The initiating commercial or quality cause remains unresolved.

### Mistake 13: Using supervisors to solve every exception

Manual knowledge replaces process control.

### Mistake 14: Activating EWM capabilities without an operating objective

The system becomes more complex without changing economics.

### Mistake 15: Counting theoretical labour minutes as cash savings

No cost or capacity is actually removed.

## The goal is less work per fulfilled promise

Warehouse optimization is often presented as a technology programme.

The real objective is operational:

> Fulfil the customer promise with the minimum necessary movement, handling, waiting and correction.

SAP EWM provides substantial capabilities for high-volume warehouse execution, inbound and outbound control, waves, slotting, labour structures, stock transparency, cross-docking, yard operations and automation integration.

The software can coordinate a sophisticated warehouse.

It can also coordinate an unnecessarily complex one.

Management must still decide:

- which service is required;
- how work should enter the warehouse;
- where products should be stored;
- how capacity should be protected;
- which exceptions deserve intervention;
- which work should be removed before it is automated.

A serious programme should not report only that workers pick faster.

It should demonstrate that:

- fewer kilometres are travelled;
- fewer units are touched;
- fewer orders wait;
- fewer picks fail;
- fewer urgent interventions occur;
- more orders meet the departure cut-off;
- total fulfilment cost decreases.

The best warehouse task is not the task completed fastest.

It is the task the operation no longer needs to perform.

### Warehouse optimization checklist for managers

- [ ] Warehouse demand is segmented by order profile.
- [ ] Productivity is measured by comparable work types.
- [ ] Active work and waiting time are separated.
- [ ] Upstream order changes are measured.
- [ ] Warehouse cost includes travel, touches, waiting and exceptions.
- [ ] Order release reflects service, capacity and transport cut-offs.
- [ ] Every wave has a clear operational purpose.
- [ ] Slotting reflects demand, dimensions, affinity and constraints.
- [ ] Relocation benefits exceed relocation cost.
- [ ] Replenishment reliability is measured.
- [ ] Pick-face size balances space and replenishment effort.
- [ ] Batch and multi-order picking include sorting cost.
- [ ] Congestion and downstream bottlenecks are visible.
- [ ] Capacity is measured by shift, hour, zone and process.
- [ ] Labour standards are used with operational context.
- [ ] Inventory differences are traced to process causes.
- [ ] Packaging variants and customer-specific work have visible cost.
- [ ] Return disposition is accelerated.
- [ ] Exceptions have cause, owner and deadline.
- [ ] Repeated manual interventions create permanent changes.
- [ ] Automation follows stable process and data.
- [ ] Automation cases include maintenance and fallback costs.
- [ ] Warehouse and transport plans are synchronized.
- [ ] SAP EWM capabilities are tied to measurable operating changes.
- [ ] Benefits represent removed cost or released capacity.
- [ ] Service, quality and productivity improve together.

### Sources and further reading

SAP currently describes SAP Extended Warehouse Management as supporting high-volume warehouse operations, detailed stock and process transparency, direct control of automation equipment and intelligent slotting intended to optimize warehouse space. SAP also positions the product as supporting lower inventory and labour cost, faster fulfilment and operational resilience.

SAP’s current SAP EWM feature scope includes synchronization with production, inbound and outbound transportation and yard logistics; labour structures and standards; inbound processing; stock ownership, physical inventory and cycle counting; waves for pick, pack and ship activities; cross-docking; value-added services; and integration with warehouse robotics.
