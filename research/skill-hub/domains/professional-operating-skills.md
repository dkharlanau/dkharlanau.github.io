# Professional Operating Skills

## Research question

What professional skills, artifacts, rules, and quality gates should Skill Hub extract from problem framing, evidence-based reasoning, assumption management, decision quality, escalation, and risk triage?

## Key practical patterns

- Problem statements should center on describing the issue, not proposed solutions.
- Evidence-based stopping can be the right, user-centered outcome.
- Psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team effectiveness; team norms matter more than composition.
- Assumptions should be documented with IF-THEN statements and converted to risks when probability/impact is high.
- Escalation frameworks should preserve context, assign clear ownership, and build trust.
- Risk management should be integrated into governance, not treated as a separate activity.

## Artifacts found

- Problem statement document (describing issue, not solution)
- Discovery sprint report with actionable next steps
- Evidence assessment checklist
- Assumption log with owner, impact if invalid, target resolution date
- Escalation matrix with tier definitions and routing rules
- Risk register with treatment plans
- Decision journal / decision log
- Meeting action plan with owners and due dates

## Decision rules found

- If a problem statement includes a proposed solution, then rewrite it to describe the issue only.
- If a team cannot demonstrate how research findings influenced design decisions, then the research is insufficient.
- If an assumption has high probability of being false and high impact, then convert it to a risk and add to the risk register.
- If a stakeholder has high power and high interest, then they must be managed closely and continuously.
- If an escalation loses context between handoffs, then redesign the process to preserve full history.
- If a risk exceeds organizational risk appetite, then escalate to senior leadership before proceeding.

## Quality gates found

- Problem statements must be validated by the people experiencing the problem.
- Research findings must be traceable to specific design or business decisions.
- Assumptions must be reviewed and validated at regular intervals.
- Escalation briefs must include: concise problem statement, severity indicator, action-oriented language, contextual background, status updates.
- Risk register must be reviewed at project milestones and when assumptions change.

## Common failure modes

- Jumping to solutions before understanding the problem.
- Treating all stakeholders as a single "user" group.
- Assumptions documented but never validated or monitored.
- Escalations that lose context, forcing recipients to start from scratch.
- Risk registers created at project start and never updated.
- Decisions made without evidence or with selective evidence.

## Source-backed notes

- USDS discovery sprints require a problem statement that centers on describing the issue, not solutions (src-501).
- GDS blog post shows that stopping can be the right outcome when evidence shows the problem is not worth solving (src-502).
- Google Project Aristotle found psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team effectiveness, followed by dependability, structure/clarity, meaning, and impact (src-503).
- NIST RMF uses a 7-step process: Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor (src-504).
- ISO 31000 defines 8 principles and a 5-step risk process: identify, analyze, evaluate, treat, monitor/review (src-505).
- PMBOK Assumption Analysis uses IF-THEN statements to convert assumptions to risks (src-506).
- Front's escalation framework uses 5 steps: Resolve or escalate → Pick the right path → Keep customer in loop → Preserve context and ownership → Track and reflect (src-508).

## Gaps / further research needed

- Kahneman-style decision quality frameworks and decision journals are well-known conceptually but thin in public operational guides.
- High-performing teams operational practices beyond Google re:Work are scattered across academic papers.
- Evidence-based management public material is often health-care or policy focused; enterprise technology application is less documented.
