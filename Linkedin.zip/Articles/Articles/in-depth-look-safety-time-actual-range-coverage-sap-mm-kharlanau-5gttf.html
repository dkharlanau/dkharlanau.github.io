Quick Guide: When to Use MRP Tools

1. Firm Zone

  • Use for: Stabilizing procurement schedules; manual changes only.

  • Where: Scheduling agreements, purchase orders.

2. Coverage Profile

  • Use for: Dynamic stock levels based on demand.

  • Where: Materials with fluctuating consumption.

3. Fixed Horizon (P1, P2)

  • Use for: Limiting automatic changes in a fixed period.

  • Where: P1 (manual fixing), P2 (automatic fixing).

4. Safety Days

  • Use for: Adding a buffer for delays.

  • Where: Critical lead-time materials.

5. Planning Time Fence

  • Use for: Preventing MRP changes within a time fence.

  • Where: Use with P1, P2, P3 MRP types.

6. Days' Supply

  • Use for: Checking how long stock/receipts will cover demand.

  • Where: Stock/requirements list for quick analysis.

Choose based on stability, flexibility, or buffering needs!

  • Minimum coverage days: Minimum stock availability.

  • Maximum coverage days: Prevents excess stock.

SAP dynamically calculates the replenishment quantity to ensure stock stays within this range.

  • MRP Area Level: Ignored by MD01n. Use classic MRP instead.

#1 Coverage Profile: Defining Stock Levels Dynamically

A Coverage Profile is used to dynamically adjust safety stock levels based on average consumption over defined time periods. It ensures your stock levels adapt to fluctuating requirements without overstocking or stockouts.

How It Works

At the Plant Level, the coverage profile is defined with these parameters:

  1. Period Indicator (M): Define up to three time periods (e.g., weekly, monthly).

  2. Number of Periods: The system calculates the average daily requirements based on this duration.

  3. Type (Work/Calendar Days): Specify whether the periods are measured in working or calendar days.

  4. Coverage Days: Multiply the range of coverage by the average consumption in the period.

Key Stock Levels Calculated

  1. Minimum Stock Level

  2. Maximum Stock Level

  3. Target Stock Level (Dynamic Safety Stock)

Example: Dynamic Safety Stock Calculation

Scenario:

  • Average Daily Requirement: 15 pieces.

  • Coverage Profile:Minimum Range: 3 days.Maximum Range: 7 days. Target Range: 5 days.

Calculations:

  • Minimum Stock Level: 15√ó3=4515 \times 3 = 4515√ó3=45 pieces.

  • Maximum Stock Level: 15√ó7=10515 \times 7 = 10515√ó7=105 pieces.

  • Target Stock Level: 15√ó5=7515 \times 5 = 7515√ó5=75 pieces.

If the available quantity is 40 pieces (below the minimum stock level of 45), the system creates a procurement proposal for 35 pieces to reach the target stock level of 75.

What Is a Planning Time Fence in SAP MRP?

The Planning Time Fence (PTF) is a defined time period during which the MRP process limits changes to planned orders or procurement proposals, aiming to stabilize short-term production schedules. It‚Äôs a critical tool for avoiding constant rescheduling that disrupts workflows.

However, PTF doesn‚Äôt work automatically with all MRP types, including standard PD (MRP), unless additional settings like firming types or manual firming of orders are applied. Without these, MRP will override orders even within the Planning Time Fence.

MRP Types That Work with Planning Time Fence

The Planning Time Fence is effective only with certain MRP types where firming rules are explicitly incorporated. These include:

  1. P1 (Manual Firming of Proposals Within the PTF)

  2. P2 (Automatic Firming)

  3. P3 (Firming and Restriction)

  4. Time-Phased MRP (Types R1, R2, etc.)

How the Planning Time Fence Works

Set the Planning Time Fence:

  • Defined in the material master under MRP 1 View ( field).

  • Specifies the number of days during which planning proposals or rescheduling are restricted.

MRP Type Dependency:

  • In PD, the system does not respect the PTF unless manual firming (fixing) of orders is applied.

  • For P1, P2, and P3, orders within the time fence are firmed or restricted automatically.

Behavior in MRP Runs:

  • Proposals outside the PTF are adjusted normally.

  • Proposals inside the PTF are handled based on firming rules (or ignored if restricted).

Example: Using P1 with a Planning Time Fence

Scenario:

  • Material: Finished Product A.

  • MRP Type: P1.

  • Planning Time Fence: 10 days.

What Happens:

Inside the PTF (Days 1‚Äì10):

  1. Existing planned orders are firmed and cannot be changed automatically by MRP.

  2. No new proposals are created for requirements within this period unless manually added.

Outside the PTF (Days 11+): MRP generates new proposals or adjusts existing ones based on demand.

Why Doesn‚Äôt It Work with PD Alone?

The PD (MRP) type by itself doesn‚Äôt respect the Planning Time Fence unless:

  • You manually firm planned orders inside the fence.

  • You configure specific planning strategies (e.g., firming types or exception messages) to limit changes.

This limitation makes PD unsuitable if you need strict control over short-term planning.

What‚Äôs the Firm Zone in Purchase Documents?

The Firm Zone is like a "no-touch" period for your purchase orders or schedule lines. It‚Äôs a time frame where MRP can‚Äôt mess with your procurement plans‚Äîno automatic changes to quantities, dates, or requirements. If something needs tweaking, it has to be done manually by you or your team.

You‚Äôll mostly see it in scheduling agreements. It‚Äôs divided into:

Firm Zone:

  • Changes are not allowed in this period.

  • Procurement proposals and schedule lines remain fixed.

Trade-Off Zone (optional):

  • Changes are allowed with caution, often with cost or approval implications.

Planning Zone:

  • Outside the Firm Zone, changes are allowed freely based on demand.

How Does It Work?

Example: Scheduling Agreement with Firm Zone

  • Firm Zone: 10 days.

  • Trade-Off Zone: Days 11‚Äì20.

What happens?

  • Any schedule line inside the next 10 days is locked. You can‚Äôt change it unless you do it manually.

  • For days 11‚Äì20, you can make changes, but your supplier might charge extra if they‚Äôve already started planning or producing.

  • Beyond day 20, MRP can freely adjust based on new requirements.

Why Use a Firm Zone?

  • Avoid Chaos: Stabilizes your procurement process and prevents constant adjustments.

  • Improve Supplier Relationships: Suppliers trust the schedules they get.

  • Save Costs: Reduces last-minute changes that can lead to expedited shipping or extra fees.
