In many IT projects, teams focus only on delivery. They do not plan how to transfer knowledge and responsibilities when people leave.

But in every real project, people leave:

  • Contracts end

  • External teams finish their work

  • Internal people move to other roles

If the handover is not prepared, the project loses time, quality, and control.

The Transition Moment

There is always a moment of change in the project:

From: Delivery team owns the system To: Support team or client team owns the system

If this moment is ignored, the system becomes risky: nobody knows how it works, where problems are, or how to support it.

What You Must Have (Even in a Short Handover)

Even if time is limited, you need minimum structure:

a) Handover Inventory

A clear list of:

  • What is delivered

  • What is in progress

  • What is not started

Use simple tables with responsible people, status, and risks.

b) Documentation (Simple but Clear)

You don‚Äôt need perfect documentation. But you do need the basics:

  • Architecture (diagram or short explanation)

  • Key processes (step-by-step: how they work)

  • Integrations (what sends data where, how it‚Äôs triggered)

  • Known issues (bugs, missing logic, ‚Äúdo not touch‚Äù zones)

  • Open risks (unfinished or unstable features)

c) Access and Ownership

Make sure:

  • The new team has working access (not just ‚Äúrequested‚Äù)

  • Every system/component has a named owner

  • Nothing is left in ‚Äúno man‚Äôs land‚Äù

d) Knowledge Transfer Sessions

If possible, run short KT sessions:

  • One session = one topic

  • Record them

  • Store and link to documentation

  • Allow questions or follow-up

e) Open Points and Risks

Create a clear log of:

  • Bugs

  • Open tasks

  • Technical debt

  • Unanswered questions

This prevents future confusion or firefighting.

üìå Tip: use 3 levels of knowledge confidence:

  1. Confirmed

  2. Assumed

  3. Unknown (flagged)

f) Decision Log

Capture what was done, and why. Especially:

  • Workarounds

  • Hard tradeoffs

  • Things not implemented (on purpose)

KPIs for Knowledge Transfer & Service Transition

Most handovers are declared ‚Äúdone‚Äù with a handshake. Then everything breaks. Why? Because nobody measured:

  • Was knowledge actually transferred?

  • Is the receiving team ready?

  • Are we operationally safe?

ITIL 4 focuses on value, risk, and transparency. KPIs help track all three.

Knowledge Transfer KPIs

üìå Knowledge is visible, useful, and not just dumped.

üìå The service is no longer dependent on the old team.
