Build Better Products with a Human-Centered Product Backlog

Managing a product backlog is like crafting a blueprint for success—it sets the foundation for everything that follows. Back in 2021, I participated in a training session that reshaped how I think about building products. Here’s my personal, practical guide to creating and managing a human-centered product backlog based on those insights.

What Is a Product Backlog?

A product backlog is more than a to-do list; it’s a living document that reflects your product’s priorities, user needs, and business goals. To make it effective:

  • Collaborate in a mixed team (Product Owner, Architect, UX Designer).

  • Involve the customer at every stage.

  • Define the MVP (minimum viable product) early with the customer.

  • Ensure backlog items are detailed, estimated, prioritized, and emergent.

Crafting a Clear Product Vision

A strong Product Vision Statement aligns the team and focuses effort. Use this template:

For [target customer], who [need or opportunity], the [product name] is a [category] that [key benefits]. Unlike [competitor or process], our product [unique differentiation].

Principle: Maximize work not done. Focus only on what truly matters to users and the business.

User Story Mapping: The Backbone of a Great Backlog

Preconditions:

Before diving into user stories, you need:

  • A well-defined Product Vision.

  • Understanding of As-Is (current) and To-Be (desired) processes.

Building the Walking Skeleton:

Start small but functional:

  • A walking skeleton is the simplest implementation of your product’s core.

  • It’s enough to test, get feedback, and adjust without overcommitting.

Writing and Refining User Stories
INVEST Criteria for Stories:
To ensure high-quality user stories, make them:
  • Independent: Can be developed separately.
  • Negotiable: Open to change.
  • Valuable: Brings user/business value.
  • Estimable: Possible to size relatively.
  • Small: Fits within a sprint.
  • Testable: Includes clear acceptance criteria.
Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs):
Derive non-functional needs like performance, reliability, or usability directly from user stories.
  • Definition of Done (DoD): Shared criteria across all backlog items.
  • Acceptance Criteria: Specific expectations for each story.
Validating and Refining the Backlog
Techniques for Validation:
  • Persona Technique: Define your users and their needs clearly.
  • Storyboards: Visualize user journeys and problems to refine your backlog.
Collaborating with Customers:
Refinement is better with customer input:
  • Validate user stories with real scenarios and quick prototypes.
  • Use tools like SAP Scenes on MURAL to co-create storyboards.
Prioritizing and Ranking the Backlog
Not all backlog items are equal. To focus on the most impactful work:
1. Estimate Complexity:
  • Focus on relative sizing (e.g., story points).
  • Build a shared understanding of complexity across the team.
2. Assess Business Value:
  • Work with stakeholders to evaluate how stories deliver value to users and the business.
3. Apply the Kano Methodology:
Categorize features into:
  • Must-Haves: Core functionality.
  • Performance: Proportional to effort (e.g., faster loading times).
  • Delighters: Unexpected features that wow users.
Delivering Product Increments
A great product isn’t just a collection of features—it’s how those features work together.
  • Plan delivery in increments that provide value early.
  • Continuously adjust based on feedback.
