Why Adding More People to a Late Project Can Be a Bad Idea: Brooks's Law

Created on 2024-08-12 13:56

Published on 2024-08-12 13:59

You’re in the middle of a project, and it’s running behind schedule. The deadline is getting closer, and the pressure is on. Someone suggests, “Let’s add more people to get this done faster!”

It sounds like a simple fix, but here’s the catch: it can actually make the project take even longer. This is what’s known as Brooks's Law.

What is Brooks’s Law?

Fred Brooks, a computer science expert, famously said, “Adding more people to a late project makes it even later.” It might sound strange, but it’s true, and here’s why.

1. Onboarding Takes Time

When you add new people to a project:

  • New team members have to learn about the project, understand the tools, and figure out how things are done.

  • The current team has to spend time training and explaining things, which takes them away from their own tasks.

2. Communication Gets Harder

More people means:

  • With more people, you need more coordination, which often means more meetings.

  • The more voices in the room, the easier it is for things to get lost in translation.

3. Complexity

Adding more people:

  • With a larger team, there are more ideas, opinions, and suggestions to consider. This can lead to delays in decision-making and execution.

  • Instead of speeding up the project, the added complexity can actually slow things down.

Sometimes, the best approach is to focus on improving the efficiency of the current team rather than expanding it. Brooks’s Law is a reminder that in project management, more isn’t always better. Sometimes, it’s just more complicated.
